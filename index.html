⚠️ Special Terms

TermWhat You Need to Know
"Healthy" (OLD definition)Until Feb 25, 2028: Must meet limits for fat (≤3g), saturated fat (≤1g), sodium (≤480mg), and cholesterol (≤60mg) AND contain at least 10% DV of vitamin A, vitamin C, iron, calcium, protein, or fiber
"Healthy" (NEW 2024 definition)Effective Feb 25, 2028: Food must contain a meaningful amount from at least one food group (fruit, vegetables, grains, dairy, protein) per Dietary Guidelines AND meet limits for Added Sugars (≤2.5g), Saturated Fat (≤1g), and Sodium (≤230mg) per RACC
"Fresh"Means raw or minimally processed, no thermal processing (except approved waxes, pesticides, mild wash, or irradiation ≤1 kGy per 21 CFR 101.95). Cannot be used for cooked, baked, pasteurized, or processed products.
"Fresh Frozen"Means frozen while still fresh (not thawed and refrozen). Specific claim under 21 CFR 101.95(b). You must document that freezing occurred while fresh.
"Good Source" / "Contains" / "Provides"Requires 10–19% of the Daily Value per RACC (21 CFR 101.54(e))
"High" / "Rich In" / "Excellent Source"Requires ≥20% of the Daily Value per RACC (21 CFR 101.54(b))
"More" / "Fortified" / "Enriched" / "Added" / "Extra" / "Plus"Relative claims requiring ≥10% more DV than reference food per RACC (21 CFR 101.54(e)). You MUST name the reference food on the label.
"Reduced" / "Less" / "Fewer" / "Lower"Comparative claims requiring ≥25% less than reference food (21 CFR 101.54(e)). You MUST name the reference food AND state % or fraction of change AND declare nutrient amounts in both foods.
"No Added Sugars"No sugar or sugar-containing ingredient (honey, syrups, juice concentrates) added during processing (21 CFR 101.60(c)). If not naturally low/reduced in calories, you must disclose "This is Not a Low Calorie Food."
"Lean" (meat/seafood only)<10g fat, <4.5g saturated fat, <95mg cholesterol per RACC (21 CFR 101.62(e))
"Extra Lean" (meat/seafood only)<5g fat, <2g saturated fat, <95mg cholesterol per RACC (21 CFR 101.62(e))
"Very Low Sodium"Requires ≤35mg sodium per RACC
"Salt Free" / "No Salt" / "Sodium Free"<5mg sodium per RACC. If claiming "no salt added" but product is not sodium free, must state "This Is Not a Sodium Free Food."
"Lightly Salted"Requires 50% less sodium than reference food. You must name the reference product.
"Antioxidant"Must identify specific nutrient (e.g., "antioxidant vitamin C"). Nutrient must meet "high" or "good source" definition AND have scientific evidence of antioxidant activity.
"High Potency"Each vitamin or mineral must provide ≥100% RDI per RACC (21 CFR 101.36(c)). You must identify which nutrients qualify.
"Natural"FDA has no formal definition! Generally means no artificial ingredients or added colors, and minimally processed. Be careful — this term is loosely regulated.
"Organic"Regulated by the USDA, not the FDA. Must be certified. "100% Organic" = all organic ingredients. "Organic" = 95%+. "Made with Organic" = 70%+.
"Gluten Free"Must contain less than 20 parts per million (ppm) of gluten

📋 Relative/Comparative Claims (Light, Reduced, Less, More, Added, Fortified, Enriched)

What's RequiredDetails
Identify Reference FoodYou MUST name the reference product (e.g., "Reduced Fat — 50% less fat than our regular cheddar")
State % or Fraction of ChangeTell consumers how much less or more (e.g., "25% less," "50% reduction," "1/3 fewer")
Declare Nutrient Amounts in Both FoodsShow actual nutrient amounts in BOTH the product and reference food so consumers can verify the comparison
Minimum Threshold"Reduced" = ≥25% less; "More/Added/Fortified" = ≥10% more. Reference: 21 CFR 101.54(e), FDA Guide Section 8, Appendix B (p. 91–94)

⚠️ Disclosure Statements Required When Making Nutrient Content Claims

When RequiredExample Disclosure Statement
Product is "Low Fat" but has Total Fat >13g, OR Saturated Fat >4g, OR Cholesterol >60mg, OR Sodium >480mg per RACC"See nutrition information for sodium content" — Must be placed immediately adjacent to the nutrient content claim (21 CFR 101.13(h))

🏥 Types of Health-Related Claims

TypeWhat It IsExample
Health ClaimLinks a food/nutrient to reducing risk of a disease. Must be FDA-authorized and backed by strong science."Diets low in sodium may reduce the risk of high blood pressure."
Qualified Health ClaimSimilar to above but with weaker science. Must include qualifying language."Some evidence suggests... however, FDA has determined this evidence is limited."
Structure/Function ClaimDescribes how a nutrient affects the body. Does NOT need FDA approval but CANNOT mention a disease."Calcium builds strong bones." ✅
"Calcium prevents osteoporosis." ❌

RequirementDetails
Product NameIf NOT 100% juice, must use qualifier like "juice drink," "juice beverage," or "juice cocktail" in the name — simply "juice" implies 100% juice (21 CFR 101.30, 21 CFR 102.33, FDA Guide Section 4: J1-J5, p. 8–9)

🚫 Common Mistakes to Avoid

MistakeWhy It's Wrong
Calling something "low fat" when it has 5g fat"Low fat" requires 3g or less per serving
Saying "sugar free" for a product with 2g sugar"Sugar free" means less than 0.5g sugar per serving
Using "prevents heart disease" on the packageYou can't claim a food prevents/treats a disease — that makes it a drug claim
Calling it "organic" without USDA certificationThe term "organic" is legally regulated by the USDA
Saying "all natural" on a product with artificial dyes"Natural" generally means no artificial ingredients
Putting "0 Trans Fat" when it has 0.4gFDA allows rounding to 0g if under 0.5g — this is technically legal but misleading
Saying "fresh" about a pasteurized/cooked product"Fresh" means raw or minimally processed with no thermal processing (21 CFR 101.95) — most manufactured foods cannot use this term
Using "reduced fat" without naming the reference foodRelative claims MUST include the reference food name and % of change on the label (21 CFR 101.54(e))
Claiming a juice product contains "juice" when it's 5% juiceIf not 100% juice, must use "juice drink," "juice beverage," etc. AND declare % juice on information panel
Not including required disclosure when making nutrient claimsIf product exceeds thresholds while claiming "low fat," etc., must include disclosure like "See nutrition information for sodium content" (21 CFR 101.13(h))
